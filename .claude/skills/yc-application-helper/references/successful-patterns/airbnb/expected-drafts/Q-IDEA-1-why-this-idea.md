# Q-IDEA-1 — "Why did you pick this idea? Do you have domain expertise? How do you know people need it?" (Airbnb worked example)

**Form text:** "Why did you pick this idea to work on? Do you have domain expertise in this area? How do you know people need what you're making?"

**Atoms applied:**
- HALE-001 (3-component idea frame: problem + solution + insight)
- PG-001 (schlep reframe — "what do I wish someone else would solve")
- PG-003 (founder 3-test: founders want it + can build it + few realize)
- PG-004 (live in the future — multi-path: heavy user / demographic insider)
- PG-005 (organic vs. sitcom — real demand vs. polite "yeah maybe")
- ALTMAN-002 (excitement test)
- ALTMAN-003 (why-now = unrealized shift)
- ANTI-009 (imprecise problem articulation — must cover why-exists, who-suffers, why-unsolved, why-now)
- ANTI-010 (imaginary demand — must cite specific evidence)
- ANTI-012 (untested problem — must show direct customer conversation)

---

## v-raw draft

In October 2007, Joe and I were broke RISD grads in San Francisco. The Industrial Designers Society of America conference had just been announced — every hotel sold out. Designers we wanted to meet were sleeping in their cars. We rented out air mattresses on our living room floor for $80/night. Our three guests stayed four days. They came back the next year.

That's when we realized: travelers don't want hotels. They want a place to sleep at the right price in the right location. When hotels run out (conferences, festivals, political conventions), travelers will accept "stranger with an air mattress" — IF they trust the listing.

The trust unlock is photos + reviews + payment escrow, all three simultaneously. Couchsurfing has trust without payment, so hosts can't professionalize. Craigslist has payment without trust, so listings are unsearchable spam. The gap is profitable.

We've been running airbedandbreakfast.com since 2008. We rebuilt the listing flow three times based on what hosts told us they needed. We launched at the Democratic National Convention in Denver — sold-out hotels, demand surge, we proved the model. 10,000+ listings now. Nathan joined as CTO in early 2008 from his Datamine exit.

We know people need this because we've watched them use it. We've sat in our living room while bookings come in. Our most enthusiastic user reaction: a 29-year-old engineer flying from London for a startup conference said "I'd been trying to find a place for two weeks, every hotel was booked, and your platform was the only thing that worked."

[Word count: 277. Within Q-IDEA-1 typical budget.]

## Why this is partner-readable

A partner reading this constructs Caldwell's "story" in real time:
- **Characters:** Brian + Joe (and Nathan implicitly)
- **Setting:** broke RISD grads, October 2007, San Francisco design conference
- **Inciting incident:** designers sleeping in cars, hotels sold out
- **First experiment:** air mattresses for $80/night, 3 guests stayed 4 days, came back
- **Insight crystallization:** trust unlock = photos + reviews + escrow simultaneously
- **Current state:** 10,000+ listings, Nathan joined, DNC launch proved the model
- **Evidence:** named user quote (London engineer)

In 277 words: complete story, complete trajectory, organic origin, named user, structural why-now. Partner can defend this in partnership meeting in one sentence: "Two designers had the problem themselves, built a v1, watched it work at conferences with sold-out hotels."

Anti-pattern equivalent (would FAIL):
> "We're transforming the future of travel by building a peer-to-peer platform that empowers travelers to seamlessly connect with hosts in their target destinations, leveraging AI-driven matching and a robust trust framework."

This anti-pattern has zero specifics, zero origin story, zero named user, zero insight mechanism. It's the over-polished fixture (test 09).
