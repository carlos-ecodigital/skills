# P-ACC-1 — "A time you most successfully hacked some (non-computer) system to your advantage" (Airbnb worked example)

**Form text (per-founder profile):** "Please tell us about a time you most successfully hacked some (non-computer) system to your advantage."

**Per HTA-003 / PROFILE-002:** PG calls this the wildcard. Strong answers can re-open borderline-rejected applications.

## Brian Chesky — v-raw draft

In October 2008, two months before our YC interview, Airbnb was three weeks from running out of money. Investors had passed. Joe and I were on the floor of our apartment trying to figure out what to do.

It was campaign season. Obama vs. McCain. Cereal aisles were full of generic brands; nothing election-themed. We bought 1,000 generic cereal boxes wholesale, designed two custom covers — Obama O's ("the breakfast of change") and Cap'n McCain's ("a maverick in every bite") — and printed limited-edition packaging. We sold them at $40 a box, marketing them as collectibles to election-news outlets.

We made $30,000 in two months. Kept the company alive. Two months later we were in YC.

[Word count: ~120. The 1-2 sentence rule is for P-ACC-2; P-ACC-1 has more room because it's a story.]

## Joe Gebbia — v-raw draft (alternate framing of same story, his role explicit)

Brian and I were three weeks from broke when the 2008 election was peaking. I designed two limited-edition cereal box covers — Obama O's ("the breakfast of change") and Cap'n McCain's ("a maverick in every bite") — over a weekend. We sourced 1,000 blank boxes wholesale, hand-glued our covers on each, and pitched them to political-news outlets as collectibles. They sold for $40 each. $30,000 in revenue in 60 days. We funded the next 6 months of Airbnb on that.

## Why this is partner-readable

The cereal-box story does the wildcard's job perfectly: it shows resourcefulness in a way the rest of the application can't. The skills tested (designing in a weekend, sourcing wholesale, marketing to media outlets, hand-assembly at scale, $30k in 2 months) are domain-independent — they predict success at unrelated startup challenges.

A YC partner reading this thinks: "These founders can ship something from scratch in 48 hours when their company is dying. That's the trait we're betting on."

Anti-pattern equivalent (would FAIL):
> "I once figured out a way to optimize my study schedule that helped me graduate top of my class."

This anti-pattern is generic ("optimize"), low-stakes (study schedule), and doesn't show resourcefulness under pressure. The cereal-box story shows resourcefulness in an existential moment.

The skill's worked example is gold for partner-simulation testing because P-ACC-1 is the highest-leverage answer in the per-founder profile after P-ACC-2.
