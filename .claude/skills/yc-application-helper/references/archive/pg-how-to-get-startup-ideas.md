# Paul Graham — "How to Get Startup Ideas"

**Source URL:** https://paulgraham.com/startupideas.html
**Author:** Paul Graham, YC co-founder
**Retrieved:** 2026-05-04
**Original publication:** ~2012, still canonical

## Relevance score per Phase 1.0 doctrine

**Score: 4/4** — foundational PG essay on idea quality, application-relevant, explicitly cited by YC partners. **Include fully.**

## Core principle (verbatim)

> "Don't try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself."

## The 3-Test (THE founder test)

The best startup ideas share three characteristics:
1. **"Something the founders themselves want"**
2. **"That they themselves can build"**
3. **"That few others realize are worth doing"**

Examples PG cites: Microsoft, Apple, Yahoo, Google, Facebook all began this way.

## Well vs. Broad Hole (THE depth test)

- **Most founders imagine:** broad-but-shallow holes (many people mildly interested)
- **What works:** narrow-and-deep wells — **"something a small number of people want a large amount"**

### The key qualifying question
> "Who wants this right now? ... Who wants this so much that they'll use it even when it's a crappy version one made by a two-person startup?"

### Examples
- **Microsoft/Altair Basic** — Only ~thousands of Altair owners existed, but "without this software they were programming in machine language."
- **Early Facebook** — "exclusively for Harvard students, of which there are only a few thousand, but those few thousand users wanted it a lot."

## "Live in the Future" (THE timing test)

> "Live in the future, then build what's missing."

Founder origins: "result of some external stimulus hitting a prepared mind." Bill Gates heard of Altair. Drew Houston forgot his USB stick. Thousands heard the same things without founding companies.

The difference: **"Their experiences had prepared them to notice the opportunities."**

Being "at the leading edge" doesn't require being a researcher pushing boundaries — **you can be "at the leading edge as a user."**

Mark Zuckerberg succeeded partly because "he used computers so much. If you'd asked most 40 year olds in 2004 whether they'd like to publish their lives semi-publicly on the Internet, they'd have been horrified."

## Organic vs. "Made-Up" Ideas (THE legitimacy test)

**Organic ideas:** grow "naturally out of the founders' own experiences." Standard for success: "The most successful startups almost all begin this way."

**"Made-up" / "sitcom" ideas:** invented by thinking directly about what could be a company. Example: a social network for pet owners. The trap:
> "When you test it, people say 'Yeah, maybe I could see using something like that,' which means zero actual demand. Sum that reaction across the entire population, and you have zero users."

## The Two Dangerous Filters

### Unsexy Filter
Avoids problems you despise. Solution: force yourself onto unglamorous problems. PG did this with Viaweb — "the problem was one that needed to be solved though" despite his lack of interest in ecommerce.

### Schlep Filter (more dangerous; tied to other essay)
Keeps you from work involving tedious, messy tasks. Stripe is PG's prime example.

> "Err on the side of doing things where you'll face competitors" and difficult implementation.

## Practical Recipes (Plan B)

When organic discovery isn't available:

- **Personal needs first:** "There MUST be things you need." Ask of previous jobs: "Why doesn't someone make x? If someone made x we'd buy it in a second."
- **Seek the unusually different:** "Try asking yourself whether there's something unusual about you that makes your needs different from most other people's."
- **Talk to everyone:** Discuss gaps in the world. Act like a consultant solving one user's problems deeply rather than imagining mass appeal.
- **Look at dying industries:** When incumbents are disdainful, opportunity emerges. HP rejected the Apple I partly because "it used a TV for a monitor, which seemed intolerably déclassé."

## The Timing Anti-Trap

> "Because a good idea should seem obvious, when you have one you'll tend to feel that you're late. Don't let that deter you... It's exceptionally rare for startups to be killed by competitors."

## The College Strategy

> "If you're a CS major and you want to start a startup, instead of taking a class on entrepreneurship you're better off taking a class on, say, genetics."

Build things as side projects with peers, not via dissertations.

## The Meta-Truth (closer)

> "Coming up with startup ideas is a question of seeing the obvious. Yet you're trying to see things that are obvious, and yet that you hadn't seen."

## Atoms (preliminary)

- **PG-IDEAS-001 (the 3-test):** Application Q-IDEA-1 must demonstrably satisfy: founder wants this + can build this + few others realize worth doing. Partners check this implicitly.
- **PG-IDEAS-002 (well over hole):** "Narrow and deep" beats "broad and shallow." Q-CO-7 / Q-PROG-6 should not boast TAM; should boast intensity of demand from named small group.
- **PG-IDEAS-003 (who-wants-this-now test):** Q-IDEA-1 / Q-PROG-5 partners look for "users who'll tolerate v1" not "users who'd theoretically use a polished product."
- **PG-IDEAS-004 (live in the future):** Q-IDEA-1 ("how do you know people need what you're making?") strongest answers come from founders who've been *living in the future* — ahead-of-curve users, not researchers.
- **PG-IDEAS-005 (organic > made-up):** Anti-pattern: "social network for X" framing without founder being part of X. Skill should detect made-up signal: founder is not personally affected, no specific user named, no schlep, "we think X would benefit from Y."

- **PG-IDEAS-007 (don't fear competitors):** Founders who anchor too heavily on competition fear are anti-pattern. Q-IDEA-2 should answer competitors with insight differentiation, not defensive moats.
