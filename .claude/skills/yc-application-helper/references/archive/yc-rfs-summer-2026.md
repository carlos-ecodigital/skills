## RFS items (verbatim per fetch)

### 1. AI for Low-Pesticide Agriculture (Garry Tan)
> Modern agriculture relies heavily on chemical pesticides, creating a problematic cycle where farmers must "Use more chemicals → get diminishing results → pay more → take on more risk." AI vision, affordable sensors, robotics, and biological innovations now enable a transformative approach. The opportunity involves building tools that help farmers "lower costs and increase yields at the same time," potentially creating "a generational company" by reducing pesticide dependency while boosting productivity.

### 2. AI-Native Discovery Engines (Jon Xu)
> Scientific discovery traditionally follows a slow loop of hypothesis, experimentation, interpretation, and repetition. Frontier AI models now perform at PhD-level on scientific reasoning tasks, enabling systems that "run closed discovery loops." Rather than copilot tools, the vision centers on "intelligent systems that can run the full design-make-test-analyze loop" independently in domains like drug discovery and protein engineering.

### 3. AI-Native Service Companies (Gustaf Alströmer)
> The shift moves beyond software and copilots toward companies that "sell the service" rather than tools. "Total spend on services is many times larger than the spend on software," with opportunities in insurance brokerage, accounting, tax, audit, compliance, and healthcare administration—areas already amenable to outsourcing and thus easier to replace with AI-driven alternatives.

### 4. AI Personalized Medicine (Ankit Gupta)
> Intelligent agents can analyze personalized health data—diagnostics, genomes, electronic health records, wearables—to deliver "highly accurate, user-specific suggestions." Two revolutions converge: plummeting costs for personalized diagnostics (genome sequencing declining faster than Moore's law) and manufacturing of n-of-1 genetic therapies via mRNA delivery. The vision encompasses the entire ecosystem supporting this care delivery transformation.

### 5. Company Brain (Tom Blomfield)
> The primary bottleneck to enterprise AI automation is domain knowledge scattered across emails, Slack, tickets, databases, and employee memory. Blomfield advocates for "a company brain"—a system that "pulls knowledge out of all these fragmented sources, structures it, keeps it current, and turns it into an executable skills file for AI." This "living map of how a company works" becomes essential infrastructure for reliable automation.

### 6. Counter-Swarm Defense (Tyler Bosmeny)
> Current drone defense systems cannot handle coordinated swarms due to cost asymmetry (Patriot missiles at $3 million versus FPV drones at $500). Bosmeny seeks founders building integrated solutions: "high-capacity interceptors," sensor-fusion software, novel non-kinetic defenses, and attacks on autonomy stacks. The insight: "drone defense is looking less and less like operating a weapon and more like running a real-time distributed system."

### 7. Dynamic Software Interfaces (Ankit Gupta)
> Traditional software enforces uniform interfaces across users. Coding agents now enable radical personalization, allowing users to become "their own forward deployed engineers" customizing interfaces to specific workflows. The opportunity involves rethinking the entire software delivery stack to support user-driven modifications through agents while maintaining underlying platform primitives.

### 8. Electronics in Space (Philip Johnston)
> Reusable rockets from SpaceX and Stoke Space dramatically increase space capacity deployment. The specific need: "inference chips" optimized for mass, thermal performance, and radiation resistance. Johnston targets experienced chip designers from major companies to build specialized silicon for space-based compute workloads.

### 9. Hardware Supply Chain (Nicolas Dessaigne)
> U.S. hardware development moves too slowly compared to Shenzhen's rapid iteration cycles (days versus weeks). The gap compounds across projects. Solutions involve accelerating part production, enabling rapid hardware iteration, and tightly integrating "design, manufacturing, and logistics" to achieve order-of-magnitude speed improvements.

### 10. Industrial Capabilities in Space (Adi Oltean)
> The vision involves developing moon and space-based industrial operations: extracting raw materials (silicon, aluminum, iron, titanium) via electrolysis and 3D printing complex structures from molten regolith. The lunar environment's lack of gravitational supports potentially makes manufacturing "more efficient than on Earth."

### 11. Inference Chips for Agent Workflows (Diana Hu)
> Standard inference chips operate in linear "prompt in, response out" mode. Agent workflows require looping, tool-calling, branching, and persistent context—fundamentally different hardware requirements. Current GPUs achieve only "30 to 40 percent of peak utilization" on these workloads. Purpose-built silicon addressing agent-specific patterns represents a significant opportunity, requiring expertise in both chip architecture and agentic execution patterns.

### 12. SaaS Challengers (Jared Friedman)
> AI has collapsed software production costs by 10-100x, eroding traditional SaaS moats built on decades of custom code. Opportunities span from pricing-undercut clones to AI-native product redesigns, bundled suites, and open-source replacements of incumbent products. The advice: "Go after the products that seem invulnerable: chip design software, ERPs, industrial control systems, supply chain management."

### 13. Software for Agents (Aaron Epstein)
> The next wave of internet users will be AI agents, yet existing software assumes human interaction through visual interfaces. Agents require "machine-readable interfaces like APIs, MCPs, and CLIs" plus comprehensive documentation for programmatic discovery and deployment. Every major software category needs rebuilding for agents as "first-class citizens."

### 14. Startups That Want to Sell to Huge Companies (Harshita Arora, Brad Flora)
> Historically, startups targeted other startups for fast feedback. Now, leaders at Fortune 100 companies actively seek AI-driven solutions. Three shifts enable this: company leaders are "out there looking for teams," AI enables small teams to "ship incredibly thoughtful, nuanced products" in months, and enterprise executives understand where adaptation is critical. Multimillion-dollar deals within the first year are now "not unusual."

### 15. Supply Chain 2.0 for Semiconductors (Diana Hu)
> Advanced chip manufacturing involves 1,400 process steps across a dozen countries over five months, managed via spreadsheets and phone calls. Visibility typically extends only to direct suppliers; higher tiers remain opaque. New American fabs and TSMC bottlenecks demand tools for real-time allocation tracking, multi-tier risk monitoring, and export compliance—areas requiring deep semiconductor knowledge unsuitable for generic ERP solutions.

### 16. The AI Operating System for Companies (Diana Hu)
> High-performing AI-native companies have made themselves "queryable"—recording meetings, tracking tickets, capturing interactions legibly to an intelligence layer. This transforms operations from "open loop" (delayed decision feedback) to "closed loop" (real-time monitoring and adjustment). The gap: no product currently connects Slack, Linear, GitHub, call recordings, and similar tools into a unified intelligence system enabling self-improving organizational feedback.
