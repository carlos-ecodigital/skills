## Part I — The Idea (application-relevant)

### Core thesis (verbatim)
> "Make something users love. If you do that, then you have to figure out how to get a lot more users."

### Depth over breadth
> "It's much better to first make a product a small number of users love than a product that a large number of users like."

### The excitement test
> "If the idea does not really excite at least some people the first time they hear it, that's bad."

### Why-Now framing
> "We like it when major technological shifts are just starting that most people haven't realized yet — big companies are bad at addressing those."

### Testing the hypothesis
- Consumer products: launch, observe behavior
- Enterprise: secure letters of intent before building
- Stay flexible: "It's important to let your idea evolve as you get feedback from users."

## Part II — Team (application-relevant)

### Founder selection criteria
Essential traits: **"unstoppability, determination, formidability, and resourcefulness."**
- Intelligence and passion rank highly
- Experience with specific technologies does NOT

### Co-founder relationship test
> "You want someone you know well, not someone you just met at a cofounder dating thing."

Pre-existing relationships prevent abandonment during difficult periods.

### Critical skills required
> "Tech startups need at least one founder who can build the company's product or service, and at least one founder who is (or can become) good at sales."

## Part III — Product (application-relevant excerpts)

### The non-negotiable
> "Here is the secret to success: have a great product. This is the only thing all great companies have in common."

### User-feedback engine
> "Literally watch them use your product. Sit in their office if you can."

Manual recruitment precedes scaling.

### Quality > Scale (early)
"Do things that don't scale" — recruit users individually, deliver exceptional support, iterate.

## Atoms (preliminary)

- **ALTMAN-001 (love > like):** Q-PROG-5 / Q-PROG-6 — partners value depth of engagement over breadth. "Few users who love" beats "many users who like."
- **ALTMAN-002 (excitement test):** Q-CO-2 / Q-CO-7 — if the description doesn't excite *anyone* the first time they hear it, that's a failure signal. Skill validation: a fresh subagent reading the description should report at least mild excitement.
- **ALTMAN-003 (why-now = unrealized shift):** Q-IDEA-1 — partners reward "why now" framings that name a technological shift incumbents have missed. Cross-pattern with Q-IDEA-2 (what others don't understand).
- **ALTMAN-004 (founder traits):** Unstoppability, determination, formidability, resourcefulness. Founder bios (Q-FOUND-1) must communicate these via evidence, not adjectives. Specific named prior projects with completion through obstacles.
- **ALTMAN-005 (co-founder pre-existing relationship):** Q-FOUND-3 / per-founder profiles. "We just met at a cofounder dating thing" = anti-pattern; "we've worked together for X years on Y" = ideal.
- **ALTMAN-006 (skills split):** At least one builder, at least one seller. Q-FOUND-2 should establish builder; broader Q-IDEA-1 / Q-PROG should establish seller capability.
- **ALTMAN-007 (LOIs for enterprise):** Pre-revenue B2B applicants should have LOIs to demonstrate demand. Q-PROG-6 / Q-IDEA-3 — LOI evidence is the substitute for revenue at enterprise stage.
- **ALTMAN-008 (do things that don't scale):** Q-PROG-1 / Q-PROG-6 — manual user acquisition is positive signal at YC stage. Anti-pattern: "we plan to scale via SEO/ads" without prior manual evidence.
