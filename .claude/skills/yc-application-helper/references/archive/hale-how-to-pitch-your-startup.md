## The Three Components of a Startup Idea

A startup idea is **"a hypothesis for rapid company growth"** built on:

1. **Problem** — must be significant and widespread
2. **Solution** — should stem from the problem, not from pre-existing technology
3. **Insight / Unfair Advantage** — what differentiates your company for faster growth

## What Investors Evaluate

- Is the idea understandable?
- Are they excited by it?
- Do they like the team?

## The Clarity Imperative

> "Clarity is the bedrock of organic growth, enabling word-of-mouth dissemination."

Without clarity, ideas don't stick or spread naturally.

## Hale's Rules — DO

- **Lead with "what" your company does** — "why" and "how" obscure clarity
- **Make ideas legible** — understandable by anyone regardless of background
- **Be conversational** — explain it like you're talking to your mom
- **Ensure reproducibility** — investors must visualize what gets built
- **Use clear nouns** — what you make, the problem, the customer
- **Be concise** — demonstrates deep thought and efficiency

## Hale's Rules — AVOID

- Ambiguity and abstract concepts
- Unnecessary complexity (intertwined ideas)
- Mystery / jargon / vague language
- "Ignorable language" — marketing speak, MBA jargon, buzzwords
- Preambles and storytelling (in the description specifically)
- Starting with technology instead of problems

## The "X for Y" Framework

Effective ONLY when:
- **X** is a universally recognized billion-dollar company
- **Y** genuinely wants/needs that model (clear unmet need)
- The **Y** market is substantial enough to support a large business

**Anti-example:** "Buffer for Snapchat" — Buffer is smaller than Snapchat, so analogy fails.

## Hale's Concrete Description Examples

- **Airbnb:** "First online marketplace letting travelers book rooms with locals"
- **Dropbox:** "Synchronizes files across your computers"
- **Lumini:** "X-ray vision for soldiers and first responders"

(All three: noun-led, plain language, no jargon, name a verb the user does, name the customer.)

## Atoms (preliminary, Phase 2 multi-passes)

- **HALE-001 (3 components):** Problem → Solution → Insight. Insight is "unfair advantage" — what makes growth happen faster than competitors. Skill must surface insight as separate component, not collapse into solution.
- **HALE-002 (lead with "what"):** Q-CO-2 (50-char description) and Q-CO-7 (what your company will make) must both lead with what, not why or how. Anti-pattern: opening with origin story, problem framing, or technology choice.
- **HALE-003 (X-for-Y conditional):** "X for Y" framework valid ONLY when X is billion-$ recognized AND Y has clear unmet need AND Y market is large. Skill should detect "X for Y" usage and verify all 3 conditions; flag if not all satisfied.
- **HALE-004 (mom test for clarity):** Description should be understandable to a non-technical, non-industry person. Skill validation: have a fresh subagent unfamiliar with the company explain the description back; must succeed without clarification.
- **HALE-005 (anti-marketing-speak):** Specifically banned: "revolutionize," "disrupt," "synergy," "innovate," "platform," and similar (corroborated by Seibel via arc.dev). Skill anti-pattern detection scans for these.
- **HALE-006 (concrete description format):** Hale's exemplars all follow: [what we are] + [what user does with it] + [optional named customer/context]. Template: "[Adjective qualifier]? [marketplace / app / service / tool] for [user] to [verb] [object]."
- **HALE-007 (pre-amble anti-pattern):** "Preambles and storytelling" forbidden in the description. Skill should detect and strip pre-amble like "We're building..." and replace with direct noun-verb-object.
