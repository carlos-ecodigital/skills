# Norway -- Contract Law

## Primary Sources

- **Avtaleloven** (Contract Act, Lov 1918-05-31 nr. 4): formation, authority, invalidity
- **Kjopsloven** (Sale of Goods Act, Lov 1988-05-13 nr. 27): sale of goods (aligned with CISG)
- **Ulovfestet kontraktsrett** (uncodified contract law): substantial body of unwritten principles developed by Hoyesterett (Supreme Court) and legal doctrine

## Contract Formation

### Tilbud og aksept (Offer and Acceptance)
- Avtl. §§ 2-9: traditional offer-acceptance model
- Tilbud (offer) is binding on the offeror once received by the offeree (lofte-prinsippet / promise principle) -- unlike common law where an offer can generally be revoked before acceptance
- Akseptfrist (acceptance deadline): if specified, acceptance must be received within the deadline; otherwise "rimelig tid" (reasonable time)
- Sen aksept (late acceptance): does not bind offeror unless offeror notifies offeree that it accepts the late acceptance -- avtl. § 4

### Fullmakt (Authority/Agency)
- Avtl. §§ 10-27: agency provisions
- Stillingsfullmakt (authority by position): a person's employment position carries implied authority for transactions within the ordinary scope
- Prokura (general commercial authority): broad authority registered in Foretaksregisteret
- Toleransefullmakt (authority by acquiescence): principal's failure to object to repeated acts creates binding authority

## Interpretation

### "Skal" Convention
In Norwegian legal drafting, "skal" (shall) creates an unconditional obligation. "Kan" (may) grants discretion. "Bor" (should) is advisory but not binding.

### Objective Interpretation
Norwegian law applies an objective interpretation standard for commercial contracts: the agreement is interpreted based on what a reasonable person in the parties' position would understand. Unlike Dutch Haviltex, subjective party intent plays a lesser role for negotiated commercial contracts.

### Supplementary Principles
- **Bakgrunnsrett** (default/background law): statutory and unwritten rules that apply unless the contract provides otherwise
- **Kutyme** (trade custom): established practices in the relevant industry supplement the contract
- **Forarbeider** (preparatory works): for statutory interpretation, legislative history is a primary source

## Key Provisions

### Avtaleloven § 36 (General Reasonableness)
- A contract provision may be modified or set aside if enforcement would be **urimelig** (unreasonable) or contrary to **god forretningsskikk** (good business practice)
- Applies to both consumer and commercial contracts (though threshold is higher for commercial)
- Court considers: terms, parties' positions, circumstances at formation, subsequent events
- **Boeteklausule (penalty clause):** LDs and contractual penalties are subject to moderation under § 36 if disproportionate to actual loss

### Avtaleloven §§ 28-33 (Invalidity)
- § 28: tvang (duress) -- physical threats
- § 29: tvang (duress) -- wrongful coercion
- § 30: svik (fraud)
- § 31: utnyttelse (exploitation) of distressed circumstances
- § 33: ugyldighet (invalidity) if the other party knew or should have known that enforcement would be against honesty and good faith (redelighet og god tro)

### Forsinkelse og mangel (Delay and Defects)
Under kjopsloven (Sale of Goods Act):
- **Forsinkelse** (delay): buyer may kreve oppfyllelse (demand performance), heve kjopet (terminate), or claim erstatning (damages) -- kjl. §§ 22-28
- **Mangel** (defect): buyer may claim retting (repair), prisavslag (price reduction), heving (termination), or erstatning (damages) -- kjl. §§ 30-40
- **Reklamasjon** (notice of defect): buyer must notify seller "innen rimelig tid" (within reasonable time) after discovering or should have discovered the defect; absolute limit 2 years (5 years for durable goods in consumer sales)

### Overmacht / Force Majeure
- No general statutory force majeure provision (unlike Dutch BW)
- Kjopsloven § 27 (control liability): seller is liable for loss unless prevented by hindring utenfor kontroll (impediment beyond control) that could not reasonably be foreseen or avoided
- Commercial contracts: always include an express force majeure clause specifying events and consequences
- Norwegian courts interpret force majeure narrowly; economic hardship alone is insufficient

### Heving (Termination/Avoidance)
- For sale of goods: kjl. § 25 (delay) and § 39 (defects) -- requires **vesentlig kontraktsbrudd** (material breach)
- General principle: right to terminate requires material breach unless the contract specifies otherwise
- Norwegian law distinguishes between heving (termination/avoidance) and oppsigelse (notice-based termination)

## Limitation Periods (Foreldelse)

| Type | Period | Start | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Contractual claims | 3 years | From date claim is due | Foreldelsesloven § 3 |
| Claims for damages (tort) | 3 years | From knowledge of damage + liable party | Foreldelsesloven § 9 |
| Absolute maximum | 10 years (20 years for personal injury) | From the event | Foreldelsesloven §§ 9-10 |
| Claims requiring special investigation | 1 year additional from discovery | Foreldelsesloven § 10 |

- **Avbrytelse (interruption):** by filing claim (forliksklage or stevning), acknowledgment, or agreed extension
- Significantly shorter than Dutch limitation periods (5/20 years)

## Drafting Conventions

- Use "skal" for mandatory obligations, "kan" for discretionary
- Boilerplate should be adapted for Norwegian law: merger/entire agreement clauses may have limited effect under Norwegian law (courts may still consider pre-contractual statements)
- Consider whether avtaleloven § 36 risk warrants specific carve-outs or reasonableness acknowledgments in the contract
- Non-compete clauses in commercial contracts: enforceable but subject to § 36 moderation if unreasonable in scope or duration
