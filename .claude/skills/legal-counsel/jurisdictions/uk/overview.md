# United Kingdom -- Jurisdiction Overview

> **Status:** Phase 4 — NDA review coverage complete. Contract law, entity types, dispute resolution, and terminology files built for English law NDA review support.

## Legal System

**Common law** jurisdiction. Law derives from statutes (Acts of Parliament), delegated legislation (Statutory Instruments), and judicial precedent (case law / stare decisis). Equity supplements common law through the Chancery Division.

Post-Brexit, EU law no longer directly applies. Retained EU law has been progressively reformed under the Retained EU Law (Revocation and Reform) Act 2023.

## Court Structure

| Level | Court | Jurisdiction |
|---|---|---|
| First instance | County Court | Civil claims up to GBP 100,000 |
| First instance | High Court (King's Bench, Chancery, Family) | Civil claims above GBP 100,000, specialist |
| First instance | Commercial Court | Commercial disputes, arbitration challenges |
| Appeal | Court of Appeal | Appeals on law and fact |
| Final | Supreme Court (UKSC) | Final appellate court |

## Key Legislation (Commercial)

| Area | Primary Legislation |
|---|---|
| Contract | Common law (case law), UCTA 1977, CRA 2015 |
| Corporate | Companies Act 2006 |
| Employment | Employment Rights Act 1996, Equality Act 2010, TUPE 2006 |
| Tax | Corporation Tax Act 2009/2010, Income Tax Act 2007, VATA 1994 |
| Data protection | Data Protection Act 2018, UK GDPR |
| Competition | Competition Act 1998, Enterprise Act 2002 |
| Financial regulation | FSMA 2000, Financial Services Act 2012 |
| Property | Law of Property Act 1925, Land Registration Act 2002 |

## Key Principles

- **Freedom of contract** -- parties largely free to agree terms; limited statutory intervention
- **Consideration** -- required for contract formation (absent in civil law systems)
- **Boilerplate reliance** -- English law contracts tend to be longer and more self-contained than civil law contracts, relying less on default statutory provisions
- **Entire agreement clause** -- effective to exclude pre-contractual representations (unlike NL Haviltex approach)
