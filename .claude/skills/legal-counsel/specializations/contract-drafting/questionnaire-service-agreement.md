# Service Agreement -- Intake Questionnaire

This questionnaire gathers all information needed to generate a complete, jurisdiction-aware Service Agreement. Questions are grouped into 12 parts. During the intake process, these are presented in 6 conversational batches.


## Part A: Party Identification (8 questions)

**A1.** What is the full legal name of the service provider entity?
*Include the entity type suffix (e.g., AS, BV, Ltd, LLC, GmbH, Inc., SA, Pty Ltd). This must exactly match the entity's registered name.*

**A2.** What is the service provider's jurisdiction of incorporation?
*The country (and state/province, if applicable) where the entity is legally registered.*

**A3.** What is the service provider's registered address?
*The official registered office address as recorded with the relevant company registry.*

**A4.** What is the service provider's registration number?
*E.g., organisasjonsnummer (Norway), KvK number (Netherlands), company number (UK), EIN (US). This is used for formal identification in the contract preamble.*

**A5.** What is the full legal name of the client entity?
*Include the entity type suffix. Must exactly match the registered name.*

**A6.** What is the client's jurisdiction of incorporation?
*The country (and state/province, if applicable) where the entity is legally registered.*

**A7.** What is the client's registered address?
*Official registered office address.*

**A8.** What is the client's registration number?
*Organisasjonsnummer, KvK, company number, EIN, etc.*


## Part B: Scope and Deliverables (7 questions)

**B1.** What is the nature of the services to be provided?
*Select the primary category: consulting, technology/IT, managed services, EPC (engineer-procure-construct), maintenance, creative/design, construction, BESS (battery energy storage system), energy infrastructure, or other. If energy/BESS/JV is selected, dedicated clauses from the energy add-on will be incorporated.*

**B2.** Please describe the deliverables or the scope of work in detail.
*Alternatively, indicate if a separate Statement of Work (SOW) will be attached as Schedule 1. The description should be specific enough to determine what is included and, by implication, what is excluded.*

**B3.** Are there specific acceptance criteria for the deliverables?
*Acceptance criteria define when a deliverable is deemed complete and satisfactory. Common approaches: written sign-off within X days, deemed acceptance if no objection within X days, formal testing/UAT procedure.*

**B4.** Are there any services or deliverables that are explicitly excluded from scope?
*Listing exclusions prevents scope creep and manages expectations. Common exclusions: training, ongoing support after delivery, third-party integrations, data migration.*

**B5.** Will the provider need to subcontract any part of the services?
*If yes, identify which parts and whether the client requires prior approval of subcontractors. The provider typically remains fully responsible for subcontractor performance.*

**B6.** What are the client's key dependencies and cooperation obligations?
*E.g., providing access to systems, data, premises, personnel, or decisions within specified timeframes. Client delays in meeting dependencies may excuse provider delays.*

**B7.** Is a change control procedure required?
*Recommended for all but the simplest engagements. A change control procedure ensures that any changes to scope, timeline, or fees are formally agreed before implementation.*


## Part C: Term and Duration (5 questions)

**C1.** What is the commencement date?
*The date on which the services will begin. This may differ from the agreement execution date.*

**C2.** What is the initial term duration?
*E.g., 12 months, 24 months, project completion. For project-based engagements, consider a long-stop date (maximum duration).*

**C3.** Should the agreement auto-renew?
*If yes: automatically renews for successive periods of [X] months unless either party gives [X] months' notice of non-renewal. If no: the agreement expires at the end of the initial term unless expressly renewed.*

**C4.** What is the notice period for non-renewal?
*Typically 30-90 days before the end of the then-current term. Longer notice periods give both parties more time to plan.*

**C5.** Is there a long-stop date (maximum overall duration)?
*A long-stop date sets an absolute end date regardless of renewals. Common in regulatory or project-based contexts.*


## Part D: Financial Terms (12 questions)

**D1.** What is the payment structure?
*Options: fixed fee (total project price), time and materials (hourly/daily rates), milestone-based (payments tied to deliverable completion), retainer (fixed periodic fee for availability), or hybrid (combination).*

**D2.** What are the fee amounts or rates?
*For fixed fee: total amount. For T&M: hourly/daily rates by role/seniority. For milestone: amount per milestone. For retainer: monthly/quarterly retainer amount. Provide a rate card if multiple rates apply.*

**D3.** What is the invoicing frequency?
*Options: monthly in arrears, quarterly in arrears, on milestone completion, upon project completion, in advance (for retainers). "In arrears" means after the period/work; "in advance" means before.*

**D4.** What are the payment terms?
*Number of days from invoice receipt to payment due date. Common: net 30 (30 days), net 45, net 60. Shorter terms benefit the provider; longer terms benefit the client.*

**D5.** What is the payment currency?
*E.g., NOK, EUR, GBP, USD, CHF. Consider which party bears currency risk in cross-border arrangements.*

**D6.** What is the late payment interest rate?
*Options: a contractual rate (e.g., 2% above base rate), the statutory rate (forsinkelsesrente in Norway, Late Payment of Commercial Debts Act rate in UK), or a fixed percentage per annum.*

**D7.** Are expenses reimbursable?
*If yes, define categories (travel, accommodation, materials, etc.), pre-approval thresholds, documentation requirements, and any cap on total expenses.*

**D8.** What is the pre-approval threshold for expenses?
*Expenses above this amount require client approval before being incurred. Common range: NOK 2,000-10,000 / EUR 250-1,000 / GBP 200-500.*

**D9.** Is there an annual fee escalation mechanism?
*Options: CPI/KPI-linked (Consumer Price Index / Konsumprisindeks), fixed percentage increase, open to negotiation, no escalation. CPI-linked is common for multi-year contracts.*

**D10.** Is VAT/MVA/GST applicable?
*Identify which tax applies based on the place of supply. For Norwegian domestic services: 25% MVA. For cross-border B2B services: reverse charge may apply. State whether fees are quoted inclusive or exclusive of tax (exclusive is standard in B2B).*

**D11.** Are there any withholding tax considerations?
*Relevant for cross-border payments. Identify whether the client's jurisdiction requires withholding tax on service fees, and whether a gross-up obligation should apply.*

**D12.** Does the client have a right of set-off?
*Set-off allows the client to deduct amounts the provider owes to the client from payments due to the provider. Commonly limited to undisputed or adjudicated amounts.*


## Part E: Intellectual Property (6 questions)

**E1.** Who will own the foreground IP (new IP created during the engagement)?
*Options: (a) Client owns all foreground IP (most common in client-commissioned work), (b) Provider retains ownership with a licence to Client, (c) Joint ownership, (d) Other arrangement. The choice significantly affects the contract structure.*

**E2.** Does the provider have pre-existing (background) IP that will be incorporated into the deliverables?
*If yes, identify it. Background IP remains with the provider, and the client receives a licence to use it as part of the deliverables. Failure to identify background IP can create disputes.*

**E3.** If the client owns foreground IP, should the provider receive a licence back?
*A licence back allows the provider to use the deliverables for internal knowledge-building, portfolio development, or (with restrictions) future client work. Typically non-exclusive and subject to confidentiality.*

**E4.** Will any third-party IP be incorporated into the deliverables?
*E.g., open-source software, licensed stock images, third-party libraries. If yes, ensure compatible licences are in place and the client is aware of any restrictions.*

**E5.** Should moral rights be waived?
*Moral rights (right of attribution, right of integrity) apply to creative works. In some jurisdictions (UK, Norway under aandsverkloven), moral rights can be waived by the author. In others (France, Germany), they cannot. This affects creative/design engagements.*

**E6.** Is a source code escrow arrangement required?
*Relevant if the provider is developing bespoke software and the client needs assurance of access to source code in specific scenarios (provider insolvency, cessation of support, material breach). An escrow agent holds the code.*


## Part F: Confidentiality (5 questions)

**F1.** Should the confidentiality obligations be mutual or one-way?
*Mutual is standard (both parties share confidential information). One-way may be appropriate where only one party discloses (e.g., client provides proprietary data to provider).*

**F2.** Is there an existing NDA between the parties?
*If yes, determine whether the agreement's confidentiality clause should supersede, supplement, or exist alongside the NDA. Avoid conflicting obligations.*

**F3.** How long should confidentiality obligations survive termination?
*Common: 3 years for general commercial information; 5 years or indefinitely for trade secrets. The duration should reflect the sensitivity and shelf-life of the information.*

**F4.** Are there enhanced confidentiality categories?
*Some information may require additional protections (e.g., restricted circulation lists, encryption requirements, separate handling for highly sensitive categories like M&A plans, pricing strategies, or personal data).*

**F5.** What are the permitted disclosure carve-outs?
*Standard: employees/contractors with need to know, professional advisers, regulatory/legal requirements. Consider whether disclosure to affiliates, auditors, or potential acquirers should be permitted.*


## Part G: Data Protection (6 questions)

**G1.** Will personal data be processed in the course of providing the services?
*If yes, a Data Processing Agreement (DPA) is required. If no, the data protection section can be omitted or simplified to a general compliance obligation.*

**G2.** What categories of personal data and data subjects are involved?
*E.g., employee data (names, salaries), customer data (contact details, purchase history), health data, financial data. Special category data (Art. 9 GDPR) triggers enhanced requirements.*

**G3.** What data protection legislation applies?
*Options: GDPR (EU/EEA), personopplysningsloven (Norway, supplements GDPR), UK GDPR + Data Protection Act 2018, CCPA/CPRA (California), LGPD (Brazil), POPIA (South Africa). Multiple regimes may apply simultaneously.*

**G4.** Will personal data be transferred internationally?
*If data is transferred outside the EEA/UK, appropriate safeguards are required: adequacy decision, Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs), Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs), or derogations. Identify the countries involved.*

**G5.** Should a DPA be included as a schedule?
*Recommended whenever personal data is processed. The DPA should cover the mandatory Art. 28 GDPR requirements: subject matter, duration, nature and purpose of processing, types of data, categories of data subjects, obligations and rights of controller.*

**G6.** Does the provider hold relevant security certifications?
*E.g., ISO 27001, SOC 2 Type II, Cyber Essentials Plus. These demonstrate the provider's information security posture and may be a contractual requirement.*


## Part H: Performance Standards (5 questions)

**H1.** Are formal Service Level Agreements (SLAs) required?
*SLAs set measurable performance targets. Appropriate for ongoing/managed services. May not be needed for short-term consulting or project-based engagements.*

**H2.** What are the key performance metrics?
*E.g., availability/uptime (99.9%), response time (< 4 hours for P1 incidents), resolution time, throughput, error rate, customer satisfaction score. Metrics should be specific, measurable, and within the provider's control.*

**H3.** What are the target and minimum service levels?
*Target: the expected performance level. Minimum: the level below which service credits or escalation are triggered. The gap between target and minimum defines the tolerance band.*

**H4.** What remedy mechanism applies for SLA failures?
*Options: service credits (most common), liquidated damages, step-in rights, right to terminate after persistent failure. Service credits are typically 5-15% of monthly fees per metric, with a monthly aggregate cap of 15-25%.*

**H5.** What is the SLA reporting frequency?
*Options: weekly, monthly (most common), quarterly. Reports should include metric performance, trend analysis, root cause analysis for breaches, and remedial action plans.*


## Part I: Risk Allocation (10 questions)

**I1.** What should the aggregate liability cap be?
*Common approaches: 1x annual fees (conservative), 2x-3x annual fees (balanced), total contract value (for higher-risk services), or a fixed monetary amount. The cap should reflect the commercial value and risk profile of the engagement.*

**I2.** Should consequential/indirect damages be excluded?
*Standard practice to exclude consequential damages (loss of profits, revenue, goodwill, anticipated savings). Consider whether specific heads of loss should be carved out.*

**I3.** What liabilities should be carved out from the cap (i.e., uncapped or separately capped)?
*Common carve-outs: death or personal injury (statutory -- cannot be excluded), fraud, IP infringement indemnity, breach of confidentiality, breach of data protection obligations.*

**I4.** What should the scope of the provider's indemnification obligations be?
*Standard: IP infringement (provider indemnifies client if deliverables infringe third-party IP), negligence/wilful misconduct. Broader: regulatory fines arising from provider's breach, third-party bodily injury.*

**I5.** Is professional indemnity (PI) / errors and omissions (E&O) insurance required?
*If yes, specify minimum coverage amount (e.g., NOK 10M, GBP 2M, EUR 5M per claim / in aggregate).*

**I6.** Is public liability / commercial general liability (CGL) insurance required?
*If yes, specify minimum coverage. Important if the provider has physical presence at the client's premises.*

**I7.** Is cyber liability insurance required?
*If yes, specify minimum coverage. Important if the provider handles personal data or operates IT systems.*

**I8.** Is employers' liability insurance required?
*In many jurisdictions this is a statutory requirement (e.g., mandatory in the UK; yrkesskadeforsikring is mandatory in Norway).*

**I9.** What run-off period should apply for insurance after termination?
*Run-off ensures claims-made policies cover claims arising after termination for events during the contract period. Common: 2-6 years post-termination for PI.*

**I10.** Should force majeure include or exclude specific events?
*Key decisions: include or exclude pandemics (post-COVID this is heavily negotiated), include or exclude cyber-attacks, include or exclude supply chain disruption, include or exclude government sanctions/embargoes.*


## Part J: Termination (6 questions)

**J1.** Should either party have a right to terminate for convenience?
*Options: (a) either party may terminate for convenience, (b) client only, (c) provider only (rare), (d) no convenience termination.*

**J2.** What notice period applies for convenience termination?
*Common: 30-90 days. Consider minimum commitment periods (e.g., no convenience termination during the first 12 months).*

**J3.** What cure period applies for material breach?
*The period given to the breaching party to remedy the breach after receiving written notice. Common: 20-30 days.*

**J4.** Should change of control trigger termination rights?
*A change of control clause allows a party to terminate if the other party is acquired by or merges with another entity. Define "change of control" (50%+ ownership, board control, etc.).*

**J5.** Should the provider be required to provide transition assistance?
*Specify: duration (typically 3-6 months), scope (knowledge transfer, data migration, parallel running), pricing (at existing rates or a transition rate).*

**J6.** Which provisions should survive termination?
*Standard survival clauses: Definitions, Intellectual Property, Confidentiality, Data Protection, Limitation of Liability, Indemnification, Consequences of Termination, Dispute Resolution, Governing Law.*


## Part K: Dispute Resolution and Governance (7 questions)

**K1.** What governing law should apply?
*This is always asked -- never assumed. Common choices: law of the provider's jurisdiction, law of the client's jurisdiction, or a neutral jurisdiction.*

**K2.** What dispute resolution mechanism is preferred?
*Options: (a) litigation (courts), (b) arbitration (private tribunal), (c) mediation followed by arbitration/litigation (multi-tier).*

**K3.** If arbitration, which institution?
*Options: ICC, LCIA, AAA/ICDR, NAI, NCC/Oslo Chamber of Commerce, SIAC, SCC, HKIAC.*

**K4.** What is the seat/venue of proceedings?
*The seat determines the procedural law of the arbitration (lex arbitri). Common: the capital city of the governing law jurisdiction.*

**K5.** What language should proceedings be conducted in?
*Should match the contract language. Specify: English, Norwegian, Dutch, German, French, or other.*

**K6.** Should expert determination be available for technical disputes?
*Expert determination is faster and cheaper than arbitration for specific technical questions. The expert's decision is typically binding.*

**K7.** Should either party be able to seek interim relief from courts?
*Standard: yes. A carve-out for injunctive or interim relief from courts of competent jurisdiction, notwithstanding the arbitration agreement.*


## Part L: Industry-Specific and Additional Requirements (5 questions)

**L1.** Are there industry-specific regulations that the agreement must address?
*E.g., financial services (Finanstilsynet, FCA), healthcare (HIPAA, helseforskriftene), construction (plan- og bygningsloven), energy (energiloven, NVE), defence (sikkerhetsloven).*

**L2.** Are specific compliance certifications required?
*E.g., ISO 9001, ISO 27001, ISO 14001, SOC 2, ISAE 3402, CE marking.*

**L3.** Should anti-bribery and anti-corruption provisions be included?
*Recommended for all agreements, especially cross-border. Reference: UK Bribery Act 2010, US FCPA, Norwegian straffeloven §§ 387-389.*

**L4.** Should modern slavery / human trafficking provisions be included?
*Required if either party is subject to the UK Modern Slavery Act 2015 (annual turnover > GBP 36M) or equivalent legislation.*

**L5.** Are any additional schedules needed beyond the standard set?
*Options: SLA, DPA, Acceptable Use Policy, Change Control Procedure, Key Personnel, Rate Card, Energy/BESS-specific schedules.*
