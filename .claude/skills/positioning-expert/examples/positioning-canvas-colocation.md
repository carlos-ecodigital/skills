# Positioning Canvas: AI Colocation to Neoclouds

A complete positioning exercise using the Obviously Awesome framework for DE's sovereign AI colocation offering to GPU cloud providers (neoclouds).

---

## Component 1: Competitive Alternatives

**Question:** What would a neocloud do for European GPU capacity if Digital Energy didn't exist?

| Alternative | Likelihood | Why They'd Choose It |
|---|---|---|
| **Nordic colocation** (Sweden, Finland, Norway) | High | Cheap power, available capacity, green narrative |
| **Frankfurt incumbents** (Equinix, NTT, Interxion) | Medium | Lowest latency, established ecosystem |
| **Self-build in NL** | Medium | Full control, custom design |
| **Stay in US** (hyperscaler or own) | Medium | Existing infrastructure, no expansion complexity |
| **Delay European expansion** | High | "Not yet — let's wait for demand to prove out" |

**Primary competitive alternative:** Nordic colocation (most accessible European alternative)
**Secondary competitive alternative:** Frankfurt incumbents (latency-optimized choice)
**Biggest hidden competitor:** "Do nothing / delay" (inertia)

## Component 4: Best-Fit Customer

**Question:** Which neoclouds care MOST about this value?

### Ideal Profile
- **Stage:** Growth-stage (Series B+) or established GPU cloud provider
- **Business model:** Selling GPU-hours or managed AI infrastructure to enterprise
- **Geography focus:** European expansion is on the roadmap (not US-only)
- **Workload emphasis:** Inference-heavy (latency-sensitive) more than training-only
- **Scale:** 5-50 MW European capacity target (too big for retail colo, too small for hyperscale self-build)
- **Customer base:** European enterprise (financial services, defense, automotive, pharma) — buyers who need/want sovereignty
- **Timeline pressure:** Need capacity in 12-18 months (existing demand or contracts requiring European delivery)
- **Funding:** Has capital to commit to multi-year colocation agreements

### Disqualifying Characteristics
- US-only focus with no European plans (no reason to care about NL infrastructure)
- Training-only workloads (latency doesn't matter; Nordic cheap power wins)
- Hyperscale requirement (>50 MW single site — beyond DE's current scope)
- Pre-committed to Nordic capacity (switching cost too high)
- Very early stage (pre-revenue — can't commit to colocation agreements)

### Best-Fit Trigger Events
- European enterprise customer asking "where will my data be processed?"
- EU AI Act compliance deadline creating urgency for European hosting
- Nordic capacity proving insufficient for inference latency requirements
- Competitor launching "European sovereign cloud" offering (competitive pressure)
- Fundraising round with European expansion in the pitch

## Positioning Against Each Alternative

### vs. Nordic ("but they're cheaper")
**Acknowledge:** Yes, Nordic power is cheaper (EUR 20-40/MWh vs. NL rates).

**Reframe:** For training workloads, cheap power wins. For inference workloads — which is where the enterprise revenue is — latency wins. Your enterprise customers need sub-5ms response times. From Stockholm to Frankfurt, that's 10-15ms of round-trip latency before your model even starts processing.

**Proof:** "Ask your enterprise customers: would they accept 10-20ms additional latency on their inference API for a lower hosting cost? The answer, for production workloads, is almost always no."

### vs. Frankfurt ("but they have the ecosystem")
**Acknowledge:** Frankfurt has the ecosystem, the peering, the established enterprise relationships.

**Reframe:** Frankfurt also has no available capacity for high-density GPU workloads. Existing providers are full. New builds need grid that takes years. And most Frankfurt facilities are air-cooled retrofits — fine for traditional IT, inadequate for 100+ kW/rack GPU clusters.

**Proof:** "NL DC vacancy is at 5%. Frankfurt is similarly constrained. We have secured grid capacity and purpose-built liquid cooling — available in 12-18 months."

### vs. Self-Build ("we want control")
**Acknowledge:** Full control is attractive if you have the time and resources.

**Reframe:** The question isn't whether self-build is better — it's whether you can afford to wait 5-10 years for grid access while your competitors serve European demand today. Self-build is a great long-term play. We're the short-to-medium-term solution that lets you start selling European capacity now.

**Proof:** "60 GW of grid applications are queued in the Netherlands. Our grid is already secured. You could start with our capacity now and self-build later if European demand justifies it."

### vs. Delay ("we're not ready for Europe yet")
**Acknowledge:** European expansion is a big decision. Timing matters.

**Reframe:** The EU AI Act goes into effect in stages through 2026-2027. Your European enterprise customers are already asking about data sovereignty. The competitors who plant European flags first will win those enterprise relationships. Grid capacity is finite — what's available today won't be available in 2028.

**Proof:** "European enterprises are evaluating European AI infrastructure providers NOW. The window to establish European capacity — before every competitor does the same — is 12-18 months."
