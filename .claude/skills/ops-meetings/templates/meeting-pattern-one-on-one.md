# Pattern: One-on-One (1:1)

## Purpose

Manager and direct report. The most important meeting in the company (Horowitz). This is the report's meeting — their agenda, their concerns, their development. Career growth, blocker removal, honest feedback in both directions.

Use this pattern when:
- A manager and their direct report need dedicated, recurring time
- Feedback needs to be given (positive or corrective) — always in private, never in group settings
- Career development needs discussion
- Blockers exist that the report can't resolve alone

Do NOT use this pattern when:
- You need to assign work (use async or Standup)
- You need a group decision (use Decision)
- You need to give feedback to a group (use Broadcast for positive, private 1:1 for corrective)
- The topic is urgent and can't wait for the next scheduled 1:1 (just call them)

## Format

1. **Report's agenda first** — the report sets the agenda. Not the manager. The manager listens first.
2. **Blockers** — what's preventing progress? What does the report need from the manager to unblock?
3. **Career and development** — not every week, but regularly. Where does the report want to grow? What support do they need?
4. **Feedback** — both directions. Manager gives feedback on recent work. Report gives feedback on management, process, team dynamics. This requires psychological safety.
5. **Manager items** — the manager's topics come last. If time runs out, the report's items take priority.

The ratio should be 70% report talking, 30% manager. If the manager is doing most of the talking, the 1:1 has become a status meeting. It's not.

## Duration

30-60 minutes. 30 min for well-established relationships with short feedback cycles. 60 min for new reports, during difficult periods, or for career development conversations.

## Frequency

Weekly. Never skip. Never cancel. If you must reschedule, reschedule — don't delete. The consistency of the 1:1 is itself a signal of its importance.

## Key Rules

1. **Never skip. Never cancel.** The 1:1 is sacred. Canceling it repeatedly tells the report their development doesn't matter to you.
2. **Report's agenda, not manager's.** If the manager hijacks the agenda with status requests, the 1:1 becomes a micro-management session. The report owns the topics.
3. **Personnel issues ONLY here.** Performance concerns, interpersonal conflicts, compensation discussions, role changes — all happen in the 1:1. Never in group meetings. Never in public.
4. **Feedback is bidirectional.** The manager should explicitly ask: "What can I do better? What's not working for you?" And mean it.
5. **Notes are private.** What's discussed in a 1:1 stays between the two people unless explicitly agreed otherwise. Trust is the foundation.
6. **Listen first, solve second.** The manager's instinct is to fix. Resist it. Often the report needs to be heard, not fixed. Ask: "Do you want advice or do you want me to listen?"

## When This Meeting Drifts

- **Manager starts assigning work** → Catch yourself. "Actually — what's on your agenda? Let's start there." Give the mic back.
- **It becomes a status update** → "I can read your status async. What do you need from me that you can't get any other way?"
- **A team-level problem surfaces** → "That sounds like a Decision meeting topic. Let's flag it for the leadership agenda. What do you need from me right now on this?"
- **Feedback gets avoided** → Prompt directly: "What's one thing I could do better for you this week?" Don't let the moment pass.

## Run-of-Show

| Segment | Time | Who | What |
|---------|------|-----|------|
| Check-in | 2-3 min | Report | How are you doing? (Genuine, not performative.) |
| Report's agenda | 10-20 min | Report | Their topics. Blockers, concerns, ideas, questions. |
| Career/development | 5-10 min | Both | Growth areas. Skill building. Role trajectory. |
| Feedback exchange | 5-10 min | Both | Manager → report. Report → manager. Specific, recent. |
| Manager items | 5-10 min | Manager | Manager's topics. Lower priority than report's. |
| Close | 2 min | Both | Confirm action items. Next 1:1 date confirmed. |

**Total: 29-55 minutes**

## Quality Check

After the meeting, ask:
1. Did the report set the agenda? If the manager drove the topics, it was a status meeting, not a 1:1.
2. Did the report talk more than the manager? If not, the manager is talking too much.
3. Was feedback exchanged in both directions? If only top-down, psychological safety may be low.
4. Was it held on schedule? If it was rescheduled or shortened, track the pattern — repeated disruption erodes trust.
5. Did the report leave feeling heard? This is subjective but critical. If the report feels the 1:1 is a chore, something is wrong.
