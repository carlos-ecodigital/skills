# Digital Energy -- Buyer Personas

Six buyer segments for DE's integrated infrastructure platform. Each persona includes demographics, pain points, decision criteria, common objections, preferred channels, and buying process.

---

## 1. Grower (Teler) — Dutch Greenhouse Operator

### Profile
- **Title:** Director/Eigenaar of a BV operating greenhouses
- **Company size:** 5-50 hectares under glass; annual gas bill EUR 500K-5M+
- **Location:** Westland, Oostland, Noord-Holland, Noord-Brabant, Limburg
- **Language:** Dutch primary; basic English; suspicious of complex legal English
- **Tech savviness:** Low-moderate; knows their operations, gas bill, land, grid connection
- **CHP background:** Familiar with CHP (WKK) cogeneration concepts. Many had CHP systems before gas price spikes. Understand dual-output energy systems.
- **Decision-making:** Owner-operator decides; may consult accountant or LTO advisor

### Pain Points
1. **Gas costs are existential.** Natural gas is 30-50% of operating costs. Price volatility destroys planning.
2. **Sustainability pressure.** Retailers (Albert Heijn, Jumbo) and export markets demand lower carbon footprint. EU ETS2 will increase costs further.
3. **Grid connection is underutilized.** Many growers have oversized grid connections from CHP (WKK) era — expensive to maintain, underused since gas price spikes.
4. **Future-proofing anxiety.** Next generation of the family business needs a viable heating strategy. Gas is a dead end.
5. **Form fatigue.** Growers distrust complex processes, long legal documents, and anything that feels like a sales pitch.

### Decision Criteria (ranked)
1. How much will my gas bill go down? (EUR per year, concrete) — and does this work like my old CHP? (digital cogeneration frame)
2. What do I have to invest? (Answer: nothing — DE finances the DEC, EUR 0 CAPEX/OPEX)
3. What happens on my land? (DEC size, noise, visual impact, land use)
4. How long is the commitment? (30 years + 5-year renewals)
5. Can I get out if it doesn't work? (Withdrawal provisions, cure periods)

### Common Objections
| Objection | Response Framework |
|---|---|
| "30 years is too long" | Gas infrastructure lasts 30+ years too. This replaces your heating source with a better one. You have 5-year non-renewal windows. |
| "I don't want a data center on my land" | A DEC is ~2,000 sqm — smaller than one greenhouse bay. Enclosed, no external noise above 45 dB at property line. |
| "What if you go bankrupt?" | ProjectBV is a Dutch BV. Infrastructure stays. Heat supply contract transfers. Recht van opstal protects land rights. |
| "I need to talk to my accountant" | We encourage it. Here's a one-page summary with the economics for your advisor. |
| "Why should I trust a Swiss company?" | Operations are Dutch. ProjectBV is Dutch. Your contract is with the Dutch entity, governed by Dutch law. |
| "How is this different from my old WKK?" | CHP burned gas to produce heat + power. CHC (digital cogeneration) uses electricity to produce compute + heat. Same dual-output principle, no fossil fuel. Your WKK gave you heat and power revenue. Our DEC gives you free heat and frees your CHP/e-boiler for grid balancing revenue (mFRR, aFRR). |

### Preferred Channels
- **Primary:** Referral from other growers, LTO events and sector meetings, Glastuinbouw Nederland network. Position DE as "digitale cogeneratie" — the next-generation CHP. Growers who had WKK understand this instantly.
- **Secondary:** Regional agricultural trade press, LinkedIn (limited), direct BD outreach
- **Avoid:** Cold email (low trust), paid digital ads (low engagement for this segment)

### Buying Process
Portal Signup (30 sec) → LOI (non-binding, 10 business days target) → Waitlist → HoT + Annex A (binding, 6 weeks target) → Final Agreements

## 2. District Heating Utility (Warmtenetbeheerder)

### Profile
- **Title:** Directeur, Manager Warmte, Technical Director
- **Organization:** Municipal or regional heat network operator (e.g., Ennaturlijk, Vattenfall Warmte, Ennatuurlijk, Stadsverwarming Purmerend)
- **Scale:** 5,000-100,000+ connected households/buildings
- **Language:** Dutch primary; English for technical and legal
- **Decision-making:** Board/management team with municipal shareholder approval; 6-18 month cycles

### Pain Points
1. **Wcw compliance.** The Wet collectieve warmtevoorziening (effective mid-2026 to 1 Jan 2027) requires cost-based tariffs, >50% public ownership (7-year transition), and designated heat company status. Traditional gas-linked pricing disappears.
2. **Heat source diversification.** Over-reliance on a single heat source (gas, industrial waste heat, geothermal) creates supply risk. Need portfolio approach.
3. **Cost pressure.** Cost-based tariff regulation means margins compress — need cheaper heat sources to maintain viability.
4. **Volume requirements.** Large networks need sustained, baseload heat supply (not intermittent). AI data centers run 24/7 — ideal baseload.
5. **Political scrutiny.** Municipal shareholders and consumers demand transparency, sustainability credentials, and fair pricing.

### Decision Criteria (ranked)
1. Heat price per MWh (competitive with alternatives: gas, geothermal, biomass)
2. Supply reliability and baseload capability (availability > 95%)
3. Volume (minimum sustained MW thermal)
4. Regulatory compliance (Wcw, environmental permits)
5. Long-term price certainty (CPI indexation, not commodity-linked)

### Common Objections
| Objection | Response Framework |
|---|---|
| "Data center heat is low-grade" | DEC server output is 50-65°C, upgraded via heat pump to 70-80°C for delivery — suitable for district heating (4th/5th generation networks). |
| "What if the data center shuts down?" | Diversified tenant base; 30-year infrastructure design life; backup heat source provisions in contract. |
| "We need municipal board approval" | We provide full technical and financial documentation packages designed for board-level presentation. |
| "We're already contracted with [gas/geothermal]" | DEC heat can supplement or partially replace. No exclusivity required. Cost comparison usually favorable. |

### Preferred Channels
- **Primary:** Direct BD relationship, NVDE events, Warmtenetwerk conference, municipal energy transition working groups
- **Secondary:** Trade publications (Energeia, Duurzaam Gebouwd), LinkedIn, joint industry working groups
- **Avoid:** Mass marketing, cold email to general inbox

### Buying Process
Initial introduction → Technical feasibility study → Heat supply framework agreement → Municipal/board approval → Detailed engineering → Long-term heat supply contract

## 3. Industrial Heat Buyer

### Profile
- **Title:** VP Operations, Plant Manager, Energy Manager, Sustainability Director
- **Industry:** Food processing, chemical manufacturing, pharmaceuticals, paper/pulp, laundry services
- **Scale:** 1-50 MW thermal demand
- **Language:** English (multinationals) or Dutch (domestic)
- **Decision-making:** Procurement committee with engineering, finance, and operations sign-off; 3-12 month cycles

### Pain Points
1. **Energy cost volatility.** Industrial heat from gas burners directly exposed to TTF price swings. Budget unpredictability.
2. **Decarbonization mandates.** EU ETS2, corporate ESG targets, customer sustainability requirements. Need measurable CO2 reduction.
3. **Process integration risk.** Changing heat sources affects production quality. Any downtime is extremely costly.
4. **Capital constraints.** Replacing boiler infrastructure requires significant CAPEX that competes with core production investment.
5. **Regulatory compliance.** Omgevingswet environmental permits, emission limits, energy efficiency obligations (EED).

### Decision Criteria (ranked)
1. Heat quality (temperature, pressure, reliability) matches process requirements
2. Total cost of heat vs. current gas-fired solution (EUR/MWh comparison)
3. Zero or minimal CAPEX from buyer (DE funds heat infrastructure)
4. CO2 reduction quantification (tonnes/year, for ESG reporting)
5. Contract flexibility (term, volume commitment, price escalation)

### Common Objections
| Objection | Response Framework |
|---|---|
| "Our process needs 80°C+ steam" | DEC delivers 70-80°C (upgraded from 50-65°C server output via heat pump). For higher temps, DEC heat pre-heats feed water, reducing gas consumption by 40-60%. |
| "We can't risk production downtime" | Dual-source design: DEC heat primary, existing boiler retained as backup. Switchover is automatic. |
| "Our procurement requires 3 competitive bids" | We welcome competitive evaluation. Our cost structure (recycled thermal energy with zero fuel cost) is structurally below gas-fired alternatives. |
| "We have a 5-year gas supply contract" | DEC projects take 18-30 months to develop. Start planning now for post-contract transition. |

### Preferred Channels
- **Primary:** Direct BD outreach, industry events (FHIF, ProcessNet), trade associations (VNCI, FNLI)
- **Secondary:** LinkedIn (VP/Director targeting), trade press (Petrochem, Voedingsindustrie), energy consultants as referral partners
- **Avoid:** Consumer marketing channels, generic digital ads

### Buying Process
Initial contact → Site assessment → Technical feasibility → Cost comparison model → Procurement/board approval → Framework agreement → Detailed engineering → Heat supply contract

## 4. Neocloud (GPU Cloud Provider)

### Profile
- **Title:** VP Infrastructure, Head of Data Center Operations, Co-founder/CTO
- **Company:** GPU cloud providers (Lambda, CoreWeave, Crusoe, Together AI, Voltage Park, European equivalents)
- **Scale:** 1-50+ MW IT load demand; growing rapidly
- **Language:** English
- **Tech savviness:** Very high; will scrutinize PUE, power density, cooling architecture, network fabric
- **Decision-making:** Founder/CTO-led with infrastructure team input; fast (weeks, not months) when capacity is available

### Pain Points
1. **Power availability.** Megawatts are the bottleneck, not rack space. Grid connections in Europe take 3-10 years to secure.
2. **Speed to deploy.** Customers (AI labs, enterprises) need GPU capacity now. Every month of delay is lost revenue.
3. **Cost per MW.** GPU cloud margins depend on infrastructure cost efficiency. Power cost and cooling efficiency are decisive.
4. **European presence.** Enterprise and government customers increasingly require data sovereignty (EU AI Act, GDPR). Need EU-based capacity.
5. **Cooling for high-density.** GB200 NVL72 requires 120+ kW/rack liquid cooling. Most European facilities aren't ready.

### Decision Criteria (ranked)
1. Available MW and timeline to delivery
2. Power cost (EUR/MWh all-in) and PUE
3. Cooling capability (liquid cooling, kW/rack capacity)
4. Network connectivity (fiber, latency to major European hubs)
5. Contract flexibility (term, expansion options, power density upgrades)

### Common Objections
| Objection | Response Framework |
|---|---|
| "We need 10+ MW and you're offering sub-25 MW" | Our Fonti pilot is 1.2 MW (4 months to operational). Powergrow is 4.1 MW. We have 70 MW expansion pipeline. Start small, prove the model, scale fast. |
| "We can get cheaper power in the Nordics" | Nordics have cheaper power but longer deployment timelines and less network connectivity. NL offers AMS-IX proximity, sub-2ms latency to Frankfurt/London, and immediate grid access. |
| "Your facility isn't Tier III/IV certified" | We build to Tier III equivalent. Certification timeline and cost are negotiable as part of the build. |
| "We need to control our own hardware" | Powered shell / build-to-suit model available. You bring hardware, we provide power, cooling, and connectivity. |

### Preferred Channels
- **Primary:** Direct BD (warm introductions via NVIDIA/Lenovo partner network), AI infrastructure conferences (GTC, SC, OCP Global Summit), AI startup ecosystem
- **Secondary:** LinkedIn (technical content, CTO-level), analyst briefings, technology partnerships
- **Avoid:** Generic data center marketing, trade shows focused on enterprise IT

### Buying Process
Introduction (conference/referral) → Technical due diligence (site visit, specs review) → LOI/term sheet → MSA negotiation → Deployment planning → Go-live

## 5. Enterprise (AI/ML Buyer)

### Profile
- **Title:** CTO, VP Infrastructure, VP AI/ML, Head of AI Platform
- **Company:** Fortune 500 / AEX-listed / large corporate with significant AI workload
- **Scale:** 500 kW - 10+ MW dedicated compute
- **Language:** English
- **Decision-making:** Multi-stakeholder (IT, procurement, legal, sustainability, finance); 6-18 month procurement cycles; formal RFP process common

### Pain Points
1. **Cloud costs escalating.** Hyperscaler GPU pricing (AWS, Azure, GCP) is expensive at scale. Dedicated infrastructure can be 30-50% cheaper for sustained workloads.
2. **Data sovereignty.** EU AI Act, GDPR, and industry regulation (financial services, healthcare) require knowing where data is processed.
3. **GPU availability.** Hyperscaler capacity is allocated to largest customers first. Mid-market enterprises face wait lists.
4. **ESG reporting.** Scope 2/3 emissions from cloud compute increasingly under scrutiny. Green energy sourcing matters.
5. **Vendor lock-in.** Hyperscaler platforms create switching costs. Dedicated infrastructure on open standards provides flexibility.

### Decision Criteria (ranked)
1. Total cost of ownership vs. hyperscaler equivalent (3-5 year comparison)
2. Data sovereignty and regulatory compliance
3. Performance (GPU availability, uptime SLA, network latency)
4. Sustainability credentials (renewable energy %, energy recycling, PUE)
5. Contract flexibility and exit provisions

### Common Objections
| Objection | Response Framework |
|---|---|
| "We're committed to AWS/Azure" | Hybrid approach: burst to cloud, sustained workloads on dedicated. Most enterprises run 60-80% predictable workloads suitable for dedicated. |
| "We don't have data center expertise" | Fully managed. DE operates the facility. Your team accesses compute via API/SSH — same as cloud, lower cost. |
| "We need SOC 2 / ISO 27001" | Achievable within the facility build timeline. Certification is part of the delivery plan. |
| "Our procurement process takes 12-18 months" | DEC development takes 18-30 months. Start procurement now, facility ready when you are. |

### Preferred Channels
- **Primary:** Analyst reports (Gartner, Forrester, IDC), direct BD via NVIDIA/Lenovo enterprise relationships, CTO roundtables, industry conferences
- **Secondary:** LinkedIn (thought leadership targeting CTO/VP), webinars, case studies
- **Avoid:** Cold email to generic procurement inbox, mass digital advertising

### Buying Process
Analyst/market research → Internal needs assessment → RFP issuance → Technical evaluation → Commercial negotiation → Board/procurement approval → Contract → Deployment

## 6. Institution (Research/Education/Government)

### Profile
- **Title:** IT Director, Head of Research Computing, HPC Center Director, Dean of Research
- **Organization:** University, research institute (TNO, KNMI, NWO-funded), government agency
- **Scale:** 200 kW - 5 MW; often grant-funded with specific compute requirements
- **Language:** English (research) or Dutch (government)
- **Decision-making:** IT committee + research leadership + procurement; grant-dependent timelines; 3-12 months

### Pain Points
1. **Insufficient compute for AI research.** National HPC resources (SURF/Snellius) are oversubscribed. Researchers wait weeks for GPU allocation.
2. **Grant funding constraints.** Capital equipment purchases from grants are complex. Operational expenditure (compute-as-a-service) is easier to justify and account for.
3. **Sustainability mandates.** Universities and government agencies have carbon-neutral targets. Computing infrastructure must contribute.
4. **Data sensitivity.** Research data (medical, defense, personal) requires controlled environments. Public cloud raises compliance questions.
5. **Budget cycles.** Annual budget allocation; multi-year commitments require special approval.

### Decision Criteria (ranked)
1. Cost per GPU-hour relative to cloud alternatives and SURF
2. Availability and queue time (researchers want on-demand access)
3. Data security and sovereignty (on-premises or controlled environment)
4. Sustainability credentials (for institutional reporting)
5. Academic pricing and flexible commitment terms

### Common Objections
| Objection | Response Framework |
|---|---|
| "SURF provides HPC for free" | SURF allocation is competitive and limited. DE provides dedicated capacity without queue. Can complement SURF for burst/dedicated workloads. |
| "We can only commit year-by-year" | Flexible terms: 1-year commitments with renewal options. No long-term lock-in required. Volume pricing for multi-year. |
| "Our grant doesn't cover infrastructure" | Compute-as-a-service is operational expenditure. Most grants cover compute services. We can structure pricing to align with grant accounting requirements. |
| "We need on-campus resources" | NL network connectivity means sub-5ms latency from any Dutch university to our facilities. Functionally equivalent to on-campus for most workloads. |

### Preferred Channels
- **Primary:** SURF/NWO networks, academic conferences (ISC, SC), direct outreach to research computing directors, university procurement
- **Secondary:** LinkedIn (academic IT directors), university sustainability offices, NWO/EU grant program collaborations
- **Avoid:** Consumer channels, aggressive sales tactics (academic culture requires consultative approach)

### Buying Process
Research need identification → Internal IT assessment → Grant/budget allocation → Procurement process (may require competitive bid) → Technical evaluation → Contract → Onboarding
