# Ways of Working

**How we operate, communicate, and make decisions.**

---

## 1. Our Values

### Intense

We bring full commitment to everything we do. Half-measures do not build infrastructure. When we take on a challenge, we see it through.

**In practice:**
- Deep focus on the work that matters
- High standards for output quality
- Full presence in meetings and conversations
- No coasting

### Efficient

We eliminate waste in all forms: unnecessary meetings, excessive process, unclear communication. Every action should move us forward.

**In practice:**
- Meetings have clear outcomes
- Communication is concise and direct
- Processes exist to help, not to justify themselves
- If something does not add value, stop doing it

### Courageous

We speak truth even when uncomfortable. We take calculated risks. We make hard decisions rather than avoiding them.

**In practice:**
- Bad news travels fast
- Disagreement is welcomed before decisions
- We bet on ourselves when the odds are reasonable
- We say no to opportunities that do not fit

### Ingenious

We find creative solutions to hard problems. First-principles thinking over "how it has always been done."

**In practice:**
- Question assumptions
- Look for elegant solutions, not just any solution
- Learn from every industry, not just our own
- Constraints breed creativity

### Relentless

We persist through obstacles. Setbacks are information, not stop signs. We finish what we start.

**In practice:**
- When blocked, find another way
- Follow up until done, not until tired
- Treat "no" as "not yet" when the goal matters
- Stamina over sprints

## 2. Communication Standards

### 2.1 BLUF: Bottom Line Up Front

Every communication starts with the key message. Context follows.

**Structure:**
1. **The point** — What do you need? What happened? What did you decide?
2. **Recommendation** — If applicable, what should we do?
3. **Key context** — 3 bullets maximum
4. **Details** — Only if asked or truly necessary

**Example (bad):**
> "I've been working on the Agriport permit application and had several calls with the municipality. They raised some concerns about the environmental assessment and wanted additional documentation. We discussed various options..."

**Example (good):**
> "Agriport permit delayed 3 weeks. Municipality requires additional environmental documentation.
>
> Recommendation: Engage external consultant to accelerate.
>
> Context:
> - New requirement emerged in Tuesday call
> - Internal team lacks capacity this month
> - Consultant can deliver in 2 weeks for €5K
>
> Decision needed by Friday."

### 2.2 Internal vs External Communication

| Context | Style |
|---------|-------|
| Internal updates | Bullets. Direct. No fluff. |
| Sales / customer-facing | Narrative. Tell the story. Summary in bullets. |
| Board / investors | Structured memo. Headlines first. Data-backed. |
| External (all) | carlos-voice.md rules. 3-pass SOP before sending. |

**Why narrative for sales:** Stories persuade. Bullets inform. Use each appropriately.

### 2.4 External Communication

For content leaving the company (customer emails, investor materials, contracts,
public-facing posts), follow `carlos-voice.md` instead of BLUF format.

**BLUF applies to:**
- Team updates and status reports
- Internal decision memos and options papers
- Sprint planning communications
- Slack messages and internal docs

**carlos-voice.md applies to:**
- Emails to external parties
- MoU/contract comments
- Investor materials and board reports
- LinkedIn posts and public content
- Meeting follow-ups sent externally

All external content follows the 3-pass SOP: (1) Draft substance, (2) Run /humanizer,
(3) Apply carlos-voice.md rules + red flags checklist.

### 2.3 Bad News Protocol

Bad news should be:
- **Direct** — State the problem clearly
- **Immediate** — Do not wait for a "good time"
- **Solution-oriented** — Bring options, not just problems

**Format:**
```
ISSUE: [What happened]
IMPACT: [Why it matters]
OPTIONS:
  A. [Option + trade-off]
  B. [Option + trade-off]
RECOMMENDATION: [Which option and why]
DECISION NEEDED: [By when]
```

## 3. Decision-Making

### 3.1 Two-Way Door vs One-Way Door

| Type | Definition | Approach |
|------|------------|----------|
| **Two-way door** | Can be reversed easily | Decide fast with 60% confidence |
| **One-way door** | Irreversible or expensive to reverse | Deliberate, require 80% confidence |

**Examples:**
- Two-way door: Which vendor for office supplies, internal process change, meeting cadence
- One-way door: Site selection, customer contract terms, hiring, major capital expenditure

**Key principle:** Most decisions are two-way doors. Do not treat them as one-way doors.

### 3.2 Enough Information

The threshold for "enough information" depends on the door type:

**Two-way door:**
- Can we reverse this in less than a day of work?
- Is the cost of being wrong low?
- If yes → decide now with available information

**One-way door:**
- What would change our decision?
- Can we get that information in 48-72 hours?
- If we cannot get better information, decide with what we have
- Never wait indefinitely

**Time cap:** No decision should take more than 72 hours of deliberation. If you are still debating after 72 hours, the decision is not clear enough, or you are overthinking it.

### 3.3 Disagree and Commit

Once a decision is made:
1. Voice disagreement before the decision
2. After the decision, commit fully
3. Execute as if it were your own idea
4. Do not undermine or revisit unless new information emerges

**Why this matters:** Speed requires commitment. Relitigating decisions wastes energy. Disagree vigorously, then align completely.

### 3.4 Who Decides

See TEAMS.md for RACI matrix. General principles:

- **CEO decides:** Hiring, pricing, capital allocation >€1K, external commitments, strategy
- **Team leads decide:** How to achieve their OKRs, day-to-day execution
- **DRI decides:** Cross-team conflicts (with appeal to CEO)
- **5-minute rule:** If reversible in a day, decide immediately without approval

## 4. Quality Standards

### 4.1 When to Be Perfect

**Perfect matters for:**
- Safety (zero tolerance for shortcuts)
- Customer commitments (what we promise, we deliver)
- Financial accuracy (numbers must be correct)
- Legal and regulatory compliance (no room for error)
- External communication (we represent the company)

### 4.2 When Good Is Enough

**Speed beats perfection for:**
- Internal processes (iterate, do not polish)
- First drafts (share early, improve together)
- Exploratory analysis (directionally correct > precisely wrong)
- Experiments (learn fast, not perfectly)

### 4.3 The Speed Principle

Startups thrive on speed. When in doubt:

- Ship, then iterate
- Ask forgiveness, not permission (for two-way doors)
- Done is better than perfect
- 80% quality in 20% of the time beats 100% quality in 100% of the time

**Exception:** See "When to Be Perfect" above. Never sacrifice safety, commitments, or compliance for speed.

## 5. Prioritisation

### 5.1 Impact First

Prioritise by impact on company objectives, not by urgency of request.

**Ask:**
- Which KR does this support?
- What is the cost of not doing this?
- What is the opportunity cost of doing this instead of something else?

### 5.2 Urgency vs Importance

```
                    URGENT
                    │
        ┌───────────┼───────────┐
        │  DO NOW   │  SCHEDULE │
        │  (rare)   │  (most    │
IMPORTANT───────────┼───work)───│
        │  DELEGATE │  DECLINE  │
        │  or batch │           │
        └───────────┼───────────┘
                    │
```

**Default to important.** Most "urgent" things are not actually urgent.

**Tests:**
- YC test: "Will this matter in 6 months?" If no, it is not important.
- a16z test: "Is this something only I can do?" If no, delegate.

### 5.3 The Non-Negotiables

Some things are always top priority:
- Company values (never compromise)
- Strategy alignment (everything connects to strategy)
- Customer commitments (what we promise, we deliver)
- Team safety and wellbeing

## 6. What We Reward

### 6.1 Behaviours We Value

- **Progress over perfection** — Shipped imperfect beats polished unshipped
- **Ownership** — "I'll handle it" not "That's not my job"
- **Direct communication** — Say what you mean, clearly
- **Initiative** — Act without being asked
- **Learning from failure** — Fail fast, share learnings, do not repeat
- **Helping others succeed** — Team wins beat individual wins

### 6.2 Behaviours That Frustrate

**Communication failures:**
- Vague updates ("things are going well" — what specifically?)
- Burying the lead (excessive context before the point)
- Problems without proposed solutions
- Surprises (bad news delivered late)
- Unclear asks (what do you actually need?)

**Execution failures:**
- Waiting for permission instead of acting
- Analysis paralysis (endless research, no decision)
- Excuses without solutions
- Missing commitments without early warning
- Over-engineering simple problems
- Gold-plating when good enough would do

**Mindset failures:**
- Protecting ego over finding truth
- "That's not my job" attitude
- Risk aversion disguised as diligence
- Slow when speed is possible
- Perfect when good is enough
- Relitigating decided issues

## 7. Working Rhythm

### 7.1 Week Structure

| Day | Focus |
|-----|-------|
| Monday | Plan the week. Sprint planning. OKR updates. |
| Tuesday-Thursday | Deep work. Execution. |
| Friday | Review. Retrospective. Prepare for next week. |

### 7.2 Meeting Principles

- **Every meeting has a purpose** — No purpose, no meeting
- **Every meeting has an outcome** — Decisions made, actions assigned
- **Default to 30 minutes** — Longer only if truly needed
- **Cameras on** — Presence matters
- **Start on time** — Respect others' time
- **End with actions** — Who does what by when

### 7.3 Focus Time

Protect time for deep work:
- Block 2-4 hour chunks for complex work
- No meetings during focus blocks
- Slack/email can wait
- Real emergencies are rare (if everything is urgent, nothing is)

## 8. Progress Over Perfection

### 8.1 The Mindset

We would rather have a team that **progresses** than one that is **right**.

Being right matters. But being right and slow loses to being mostly right and fast.

**Why:**
- Markets do not wait for perfect answers
- Learning comes from doing, not planning
- Momentum builds on itself
- Perfect plans fail on contact with reality anyway

### 8.2 In Practice

- Ship the MVP
- Get feedback early
- Iterate based on reality
- Celebrate shipped, not theorised
- Measure progress weekly, not quarterly

## 9. Summary Principles

1. **BLUF** — Lead with the point
2. **Two-way doors move fast** — Most decisions are reversible
3. **Disagree and commit** — Debate, then align
4. **Progress over perfection** — Ship and iterate
5. **Impact over urgency** — Important beats urgent
6. **Own it** — If you see a problem, you own it
7. **Speed as default** — Slow only when irreversible
8. **Bad news fast** — With solutions attached
9. **Outcomes over activity** — Results matter, not effort
10. **No surprises** — Communicate early and often
