# Contact Targeting — Multi-Threaded Buying Committee Mapping

## Overview

Effective B2B outreach requires engaging multiple stakeholders in the buying committee. This guide covers role identification, title mapping, research methods, and geographic considerations for the Benelux region.

---

## The Three Roles

### 1. Champion (Technical Advocate)

**Definition**: The person who will internally advocate for the solution. They understand the technical problem, see the value, and will push the deal forward within the organization.

**Characteristics**:
- Directly experiences the pain point (e.g., managing infrastructure, running workloads)
- Has influence but not always budget authority
- Will be the primary user or overseer of the solution
- Attends technical conferences and reads industry publications
- Often reachable via technical communities and events

**Typical Titles by Company Size**:

| Company Size | Common Titles |
|---|---|
| Enterprise (>5,000 emp) | VP of Infrastructure, Director of Cloud Operations, Head of Platform Engineering, Senior Director IT Operations, Chief Architect |
| Upper Mid-Market (1,000-5,000) | Head of IT, IT Director, Infrastructure Manager, Cloud Architect, DevOps Lead, Head of Engineering |
| Mid-Market (250-1,000) | IT Manager, System Administrator (senior), Infrastructure Lead, Technical Lead, Head of ICT |

### 2. Economic Buyer (Budget Authority)

**Definition**: The person who controls or approves the budget. They care about ROI, total cost of ownership, strategic alignment, and risk mitigation.

**Characteristics**:
- Signs off on expenditures above a threshold
- Thinks in terms of business outcomes, not technical specifications
- Concerned with vendor stability, compliance, and strategic fit
- May delegate technical evaluation to the Champion
- Harder to reach cold; often engaged after Champion interest

**Typical Titles by Company Size**:

| Company Size | Common Titles |
|---|---|
| Enterprise | CIO, CTO, CFO (for large deals), VP of Technology, Chief Digital Officer |
| Upper Mid-Market | CIO, CTO, Director of IT, VP of Operations, CFO |
| Mid-Market | Managing Director (Directeur), CFO, Head of Finance & IT, Owner/DGA (Directeur-Grootaandeelhouder) |

### 3. Blocker (Procurement / Legal / Security)

**Definition**: The person who can slow down or stop a deal through procurement processes, security reviews, or legal objections. Not necessarily opposed, but must be satisfied.

**Characteristics**:
- Evaluates vendor risk, compliance, and contractual terms
- May enforce procurement frameworks or preferred vendor lists
- Security team members evaluate data protection and access controls
- Legal reviews contracts, SLAs, and liability
- In government/defense, procurement follows formal tender processes

**Typical Titles by Company Size**:

| Company Size | Common Titles |
|---|---|
| Enterprise | CISO, Head of Procurement, Chief Procurement Officer, Head of Vendor Management, Legal Counsel, DPO |
| Upper Mid-Market | IT Security Manager, Procurement Manager, Legal Counsel, Compliance Officer |
| Mid-Market | Office Manager (often handles procurement), External Legal Advisor, Accountant/Controller |

## Per-Tier Mapping Depth

### Tier 1 Accounts (Top Priority — Largest Companies)
- **3 contacts minimum**: Champion + Economic Buyer + Blocker
- Full buying committee mapping
- Detailed rationale per contact
- Verify all contact data points
- Include LinkedIn URLs for all three
- Email discovery for Champion (minimum)

### Tier 2 Accounts (Secondary Priority)
- **2 contacts minimum**: Champion + Economic Buyer
- Rationale for both contacts
- LinkedIn URLs for both
- Email discovery for Champion (best effort)

### Tier 3 Accounts (Tertiary / Long-Tail)
- **1 contact minimum**: Champion (or most relevant technical decision-maker)
- Brief rationale
- LinkedIn URL
- Email discovery optional

## Research Waterfall

When identifying and researching contacts, follow this sequence. Stop when you have sufficient confidence.

### Step 1: Company Website
- "About Us" / "Over Ons" / leadership pages
- Press releases mentioning leadership changes
- Blog posts or technical articles by staff
- Event speaking engagements listed on site
- "Team" pages (common in mid-market/startups)
- Annual reports (often list board and management team)

### Step 2: LinkedIn
- Company page → "People" tab
- Filter by title keywords: CIO, CTO, IT Director, Infrastructure, Cloud, etc.
- Check connections and endorsements for seniority signals
- Review activity for technology interest signals
- Note: In NL, many professionals have well-maintained LinkedIn profiles
- Note: BE/LU professionals may use LinkedIn less actively

### Step 3: Press and Media
- Google News search: "[Company name] + CIO/CTO/IT Director"
- Trade publication interviews (Computable.nl, CIO Magazine NL, Datanews.be)
- Award announcements (CIO of the Year, etc.)
- Conference speaker announcements

### Step 4: Conferences and Events
- Speaker lists from relevant conferences
- Attendee lists (when available)
- Panel discussions and roundtable participants
- Key events by vertical:
  - Healthcare: Zorg & ICT, HIMSS Europe
  - Finance: Money20/20, Sibos, Holland FinTech
  - Logistics: Logistica, Transport & Logistics
  - Defense: NIDV Symposium, Euronaval
  - Agriculture: Agritechnica, Potato Europe
  - Horticulture: GreenTech, HortiContact

### Step 5: Chamber of Commerce (KVK / KBO)
- KVK extract shows authorized signatories (tekenbevoegden)
- Board members (bestuurders) listed in Handelsregister
- UBO register for ultimate beneficial owners
- KBO/BCE (Belgium) for similar data
- Chambre de Commerce (Luxembourg)

### Step 6: Annual Reports
- Board of Directors and Management Board listings
- Organizational charts (sometimes included)
- Strategic priorities mentioning technology leaders
- Available via jaarverslagenzorg.nl (healthcare), company websites, or KVK filings

## Email Discovery Waterfall

### Pattern Detection
1. Check company website for any listed email addresses to detect the pattern
2. Common patterns for Benelux:
   - `j.devries@company.nl` (initial.surname — very common in NL)
   - `jan.devries@company.nl` (firstname.surname)
   - `jdevries@company.nl` (initialsurname)
   - `jan@company.nl` (firstname only — smaller companies)
   - `info@company.nl` (generic — last resort)

### Verification Methods
- Check if pattern works via SMTP verification (tool-dependent)
- LinkedIn profile sometimes shows email in contact info (requires connection or InMail)
- Google search: `"email" site:company.nl` or `"@company.nl"`
- Press releases sometimes include contact emails
- Conference presentations sometimes include speaker emails
- GitHub profiles (for technical contacts) may list email
- Academic publications (for healthcare/research) often include author emails

### Important Notes
- Never guess or fabricate email addresses
- Mark confidence level: Verified, Pattern-Based, Unverified
- GDPR applies — only collect business email addresses with legitimate interest basis
- If email cannot be found, LinkedIn URL is acceptable as primary contact method

## Power Mapping Indicators

Signals that indicate decision-making authority:

### Strong Indicators
- Listed as board member or management team on website
- Quoted in press releases about technology decisions
- Speaks at conferences on behalf of the company
- Signs procurement contracts (visible in tender databases)
- Has direct reports in infrastructure/cloud teams (LinkedIn)
- Title includes "Chief," "VP," "Head of," or "Directeur"

### Moderate Indicators
- Publishes thought leadership on company blog
- Active on LinkedIn discussing technology strategy
- Listed in annual report management overview
- Participates in industry association boards
- Job listings reporting to this person (indicates seniority)

### Weak Indicators (Supplement Only)
- Large LinkedIn network
- Multiple endorsements for relevant skills
- Long tenure at company
- Previous titles at other organizations

## Contact Validation Checklist

Before including a contact in the output:

- [ ] Name is correctly spelled (check multiple sources)
- [ ] Title is current (not a previous role)
- [ ] Person is still at the company (check LinkedIn for "Present" indicator)
- [ ] LinkedIn URL is valid and points to the correct person
- [ ] Role matches the assigned buying committee position
- [ ] Contact is at the correct entity (not a subsidiary or parent)
- [ ] For NL: Check Dutch naming conventions (tussenvoegsel: van, van der, de, etc.)
- [ ] For BE: Check if Flemish or Walloon (affects language preference)
- [ ] For LU: Check if Luxembourgish, French, German, or English speaker

## Writing Good Rationale

The "Rationale" field explains WHY this specific person was selected. Good rationale:

### Good Examples
- "CIO overseeing digital transformation program; quoted in Computable discussing cloud migration plans for 2026"
- "Head of Infrastructure responsible for 3 data centers; LinkedIn shows Azure and VMware expertise"
- "CFO with technology background; previously CTO at [company]; likely to understand compute investment ROI"
- "CISO responsible for security architecture; speaker at One Conference 2025 on zero-trust infrastructure"

### Bad Examples (Avoid)
- "Senior person at the company" (too vague)
- "Found on LinkedIn" (not a rationale)
- "Probably responsible for IT" (speculative without evidence)
- "CIO" (just restating the title)

### Rationale Formula
`[Role] + [Evidence of relevance] + [Why they matter for this engagement]`

## NL/BE/LU Geographic Considerations

### Netherlands
- Business culture is direct and egalitarian; titles matter less than in some countries
- Many C-level executives are accessible via LinkedIn
- "DGA" (Directeur-Grootaandeelhouder) is common in mid-market — means owner-director
- Randstad region (Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, Utrecht) is the business hub
- Eindhoven (Brainport) is the technology hub
- Business language: Dutch internally, English widely accepted for business

### Belgium
- More formal business culture than NL
- Flemish (north) vs. Walloon (south) divide affects language preference
- Brussels is bilingual (French/Dutch) but French-dominant in business
- Decision-making can be more hierarchical than NL
- Larger companies often have French and Dutch management tracks
- Check company HQ location to determine primary language

### Luxembourg
- Highly international; business conducted in French, German, English, and Luxembourgish
- Financial sector is heavily international (many non-Luxembourgish executives)
- Small country — networking and personal connections matter more
- Many companies have cross-border operations with BE/DE/FR
- EU institutions create a unique government/institutional segment
- NSPA (NATO) creates defense opportunities unique to LU

### Cross-Border Considerations
- Some companies operate across NL/BE/LU as Benelux entities
- HQ location determines primary regulatory regime
- Check for Benelux-wide roles vs. country-specific roles
- Language preference should be noted in contact record
- Time zones are the same (CET/CEST) — no scheduling issues
